DATA SCIENCE WORKFLOW

Data science is the practice of turning raw data into useful insight, predictions,
or decisions.

Typical workflow:
1. Frame the question.
2. Gather data.
3. Clean and prepare data.
4. Explore patterns.
5. Build models when needed.
6. Communicate findings clearly.

Framing the question:
Strong data projects start with a clear business or learning question.
Example: "Which students are likely to need extra support next week?"

Data collection:
Data can come from databases, APIs, spreadsheets, surveys, application logs, or
third-party datasets. It is important to understand how the data was collected and
what might be missing.

Data cleaning:
Cleaning often includes:
- handling missing values,
- fixing inconsistent categories,
- converting data types,
- removing duplicates,
- checking outliers,
- standardizing units and timestamps.

Exploratory data analysis:
EDA helps us understand distributions, relationships, anomalies, and early
hypotheses before modeling. Common tools include summary statistics, histograms,
scatter plots, correlation checks, and grouped comparisons.

Feature preparation:
Raw columns are often not ideal for modeling. A data scientist may normalize
numeric fields, encode categories, aggregate records, or create lag features.

Visualization:
Charts help communicate patterns quickly. Good visualizations answer a question
clearly and avoid unnecessary clutter.

Modeling:
Not every data science problem needs machine learning. Sometimes a dashboard,
descriptive analysis, or an experiment is enough. When modeling is appropriate,
you should compare a simple baseline against more advanced options.

Communication:
A successful data science result explains:
- what question was asked,
- what data was used,
- what assumptions were made,
- what the main findings are,
- what actions should follow.

Common tools:
- Python
- pandas
- NumPy
- matplotlib
- seaborn
- scikit-learn
- Jupyter notebooks
- SQL

Important principle:
Data science is not just about models. It is about making trustworthy decisions
with evidence and explaining those decisions clearly to other people.
